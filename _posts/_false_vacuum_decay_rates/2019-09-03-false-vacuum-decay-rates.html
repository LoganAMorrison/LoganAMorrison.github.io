Introduction

In a scalar field theory, it is possible that multiple local minima of the scalar potential exist. It's possible that the scalar field could be stuck in one of the local minima which isn't the true minimum of the theory. Classically, the scalar field would happily stay at this local minimum forever if let unperturbed. However, quantum mechanics gives rise to the possibility for the particle to tunnel out of the local minimum and escape to the global minimum. In fact, it is the case that, in the SM, our Higgs boson is actually at a local minimum in its potential and not at the global minimum. One might ask the following question: if a particle is stuck in a local minimum of its potential, over what time scale will the particle tunnel to the global minimum? This is the question we would like to answer. We will follow the seminal work of Sidney Coleman in his famous papers Fate of the false vacuum: Semiclassical theory and Fate of the false vacuum. II. First quantum corrections. The types of potentials we will look are look like the following

import PyPlot; const plt=PyPlot;

In this plot, we can see there exist a local minimum at $\phi=0$ but a global minimum away from zero. Thus, we would like to know how long a particle can survive in this local minimum before it tunnels to the globabl minimum. For terminology, we will call the local, non-global minimum the false vacuum and the global minimum the true vacuum. We will refer to the tunning proceedure as the particcle decaying from the false to the true vaccuum.

Computing the False Vacuum Decay Rate

1+1 Dimesional Field Theory

To get our feet wet, we will start with a simple example of a scalar field moving in 1-dimension under a potential $V(x)$. We suppose the particle is initially in a position eigenstate located at $x_{i}$ at time $-T/2$ and we will investigate the probability of the particle transitioning to the position $x_{f}$ at time $T/2$. This amplitude is given by $\langle x_{f}|e^{-iHT}|x_{i}\rangle$. We will euclideanize this expression by wick rotating to imaginary time, $\tau=-it$. After euclideanizing, we can write the amplitude as a path integral: $\begin{align} \langle x_{f}|e^{-HT}|x_{i}\rangle &= \mathcal{N}\int\mathcal{D}x \ e^{-S_{E}} \end{align}$ where $S_{E}$ is the euclidean action and $\mathcal{N}$ is a normalization factor and we have redefined $T \to -iT$. The euclidean action will be given by $\begin{align} S_{E}[x(t)] &= \int_{-T/2}^{T/2}dt\left[\dfrac{1}{2} \left(\dfrac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + V(x)\right] \end{align}$ If we expand the action around a fixed trajectory $\bar{x}$ obeying the boundary conditions $\bar{x}(-T/2) = x_{i}$ and $\bar{x}(T/2) = x_{f}$, then we find that: $\begin{align} S_{E}[\bar{x} + \delta x] &= S_{E}[\bar{x}(t)] + \int dt_{1}\dfrac{\delta S_{E}}{\delta x(t_1)}\delta x(t_1)\\ &\qquad + \dfrac{1}{2}\int dt_{1}dt_{2}\dfrac{\delta^2 S_{E}}{\delta x(t_1)\delta x(t_2)} \delta x(t_1)\delta x(t_2) + \mathrm{O}(\delta x^3) \end{align}$ where the functional determinants are given by: $\begin{align} \dfrac{\delta S_{E}}{\delta x(t_1)} &= -\dfrac{d^2\bar{x}}{dt^2} + \dfrac{dV}{dx}&, \dfrac{\delta^2 S_{E}}{\delta x(t_1)\delta x(t_2)} &= -\dfrac{d^2}{dt^2} + \dfrac{dV^2}{dx^2} \end{align}$ We know that if we integrate over the fluctuation field $\delta x$ that we will obtain a determinant of the second variation of the action. That is: $\begin{align} \int\mathcal{D}x \ e^{-S_{E}} = e^{-S_{E}[\bar{x}]} \det[-\partial_{t}^2 + V'']^{-1/2} + \cdots \end{align}$ where the $\cdots$ represent higher order terms coming from higher order pieces in the expansion of the action. We need to be slightly careful with the expression. We know that a determinant is the product over all the eigen values of the operator.
